Part 3:
Levels of Probation/Parole Supervision

Evaluations of regular probation supervision have not been very encouraging. Peter Greenwood (1996) concluded that "an overworked probation officer who sees a client only once a month has little ability either to monitor the client's behavior or to exert much of an influence over his life." Lipsey (1992) found that for youth with multiple risk factors (e.g., several prior arrests, arrests at an early age, drug or gang involvement, parental problems), "probation as usual" was not an effective option. (OJJDP, 1999, page)

I started teaching in 1970. Back then, when I taught about what probation and parole officers do, I was referring to a job in which officers counseled their clients, helped them find work, and, in many other ways, directly intervened in their lives. This situation has changed radically over the years as the size of caseloads has increased. 

Statements such as "The shortage of probation officers has handicapped the Probation Department's ability to protect the public" (The Antonovich Reports, 1997, not found on the web as of December 2005) may be heard in jurisdictions across the United States and in other countries. The reasons for this shortage include declining state budgets, the undesirable salaries offered probation/parole officers, and the excessive caseloads which are being assigned to them.

Courts with juvenile jurisdiction handled nearly 1.8 million delinquency cases in 1998. Probation supervision was the most severe disposition in about 38% (665,500) of all delinquency cases. The number of cases placed on probation grew 56% between 1989 and 1998. (Black, 2001, p. 1)

Today, probation and parole officers, particularly those in larger urban areas, are more accurately referred to as "brokers of services." They are situated between their clients and the services available in the community.

Depending upon the community, the services may include individual and family counseling for a variety of issues (i.e., substance abuse, parenting skills), anger management and conflict resolution training, life skills (i.e., learning how to budget one's money, shop for values, take care of an apartment), education (i.e., completing an Adult Basic Education and G.E.D. degree), literacy training, child abuse counseling and abuse prevention, job training and placement, housing assistance, food banks, and other services as the community allows.

After determining the kind of help their clients need, probation/parole officers match their needs with appropriate social service agencies or community-based treatment programs. Their job has become one of brokering community services and monitoring their clients' progress and activities in them.

Clients are also required to contact their probation/parole officers. The extent of their contact varies according to the level of supervision each client needs. Today, that need is determined, in part, by the kind of offender they are (i.e., the type of offense committed, prior record, etc.) and their willingness to respond positively to treatment.

Field Note: A Chief Probation and Parole Officer with 28 years of experience in the field said "It used to be that all we had available for our clients was probation, parole, or institutionalization. Now we have various levels of supervision within probation and parole. The increase in these alternatives within supervision has been a positive development. And we also have alternative programs in the community such as community service, electronic monitoring of clients, and other things.

"In the past, we didn't specialize in our probation/parole services. Now we have officers who specialize in youths who have been abused, those with substance abuse problems, clients who are dangerous felons, sex offenders, and those who are gang members.

"I've found there are three kinds of clients. On just about anyone's caseload there are situational offenders - they did what they did because of the immediate circumstances. They're sorry they did what they did so they need very little supervision. Then there are the clients who have no interest in changing no matter what we do for them. And it's not uncommon to spend a lot of energy trying to help them. Unfortunately, that takes time away from dealing with the third group - clients who could go either way. We need to be sure we focus enough attention on them."

Regular supervision, the least restrictive level of supervision, requires that clients either call or mail their particulars to their probation or parole officer - sometimes only once a month. The particulars include reporting as to where they are working and living, who they are living with, and how they are doing.

The next level up in supervision requires contact with the probation/parole officer once a week. Sometimes referred to as enhanced probation or intensive probation, it is sometimes used with more difficult cases. Visits of more than once a week are required of clients who are on intensive probation and may include home visits and visits to the clients' work place.   

Field Note: The parole officer told me "There is a State-required 'Continuum of Supervision Strategies' that we have to use. It increases treatment or punishments gradually and concludes with revocation and incarceration."

The Day Report Center is an example of an intensive form of supervision. Requiring daily participation, it is used by some probation and parole officers with gang- and non-gang clients. We'll look more closely at it in the next section.
