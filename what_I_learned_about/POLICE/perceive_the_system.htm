Part 7:
Police Perceptions of "The System"

Field Note: I spent a few days with a police officer who, for the past twenty years, has been working juveniles. He believes "The criminal justice system is going down in flames."

In a community of nearly 600,000 an experienced gang detective said "I have the best position in the (police) department because of the autonomy I have. I track juvenile delinquents, visit their homes with their probation officers, and do many other things. I am also the liaison between my department and juvenile services in our community.

"But, to be honest, the weak link in (our) system of justice is the juvenile justice system ... by far. By far! Working with the juvenile justice system is the most frustrating part of my job. They say they are going to monitor and supervise the kids, then they don't. They're simply overwhelmed by the number of kids sent (their way)."

All the police I interviewed were frustrated about how the justice system dealt with gangs and their members. What follows are comments which reflect how most officers felt about the entire system as well as some of its specific components.

Police Perceptions of the Overall System of Justice

Field Note: A gang unit officer said "The justice system is a paper tiger."

Overall, the police I observed viewed the justice system as too lenient and therefore ineffective as a deterrent for the offenders who pass through it. The most consistent concern was about how juveniles were dealt with in the courts, particularly those who were involved in gang activity.

Field Note: A gang unit supervisor said "The entire system coddles offenders and soft-shoes around penalizing them or holding them responsible for their delinquency."

 Field Note: A gang unit officer concurred saying "Gangs could be eradicated, but we're just pussy-footing around the issue."

Their concern with juveniles was related to their belief that juveniles treated effectively by the courts early in their lives would be less likely to become involved with gangs.

Some expressed the belief that if police, prosecutors, judges, probation and parole officers, and community treatment staff would simply do what it is that they were hired to do, the gang situation would not be as serious as it currently is. The following sections speak to some of their frustration.

Field Note: "If everyone in the criminal justice system would simply do their job, there would be no problem. We're our own worst enemy," a gang unit officer said.

Police Perceptions of Prosecutors

Field Note: A gang unit Sergeant suggested "All this out here is a game. I don't think a police officer could do his job if he took all this seriously. The courts are too lenient and prosecutors don't want to file cases they have no guarantee of winning because they're afraid of losing a case. They'd lose their job!"

The first contact police formally have with the courts is with prosecutors. They present their cases to prosecutors in hopes of being able to get their suspects in front of a judge. Officers working with prosecutors who practiced gang-related vertical prosecution expressed greater satisfaction with prosecutors and the courts than those working with prosecutors who simply assigned cases to the next available deputy prosecutor (a process called "rotation").

Exceptions to this finding were related to the inability of prosecutors to "make a case" using gang-related legislation. Not only are gang cases difficult to prosecute, the end result often produces frustration in the police who bring the cases to the prosecutor's attention in the first place.

Field Note: The gang unit officers told me there is a team of prosecutors who work gang cases. "But they drop lots of cases" (slang for plea bargaining them), one said, "and that's very frustrating."

Officer satisfaction with a prosecutor's office appeared to be a result of the belief that vertically prosecuted gang members were more likely to face conviction and certain punishment (or treatment). In addition, using vertical prosecution of gang members, gang unit officers have contact with only certain prosecutors - the gang prosecutors. Being able to build upon this relationship appears to produce greater satisfaction among officers - they know which prosecutors their cases are going to, how they are likely to be handled, and the prosecutors know the officers better than they would under a rotation system of case assignment. Similarly, gang unit officers know which cases to avoid because they know the prosecutor probably won't take them to court.

Field Note: Talking about the prosecutor, a gang unit member said "All we ask is that, when we bring you a case, would you please prosecute?!"

The following Field Note is indicative of police gang officer frustration from working in a rotational prosecution environment. In that setting cases are bandied about in the prosecutor's office and may, in the end, be lost in court (i.e., dismissed, result in an acquittal, end with a lenient plea bargain) due to the prosecutor's lack of familiarity with the case. 

Field Note: About working with prosecutors, the gang unit member said "The non-homicide cases are handed out in rotation through the D.A.'s [district attorney's or prosecutor's] office with people who often have little experience in the job. We have to start from ground zero with a new prosecutor every time we want to prosecute a gang member for murder. It's a real hassle. It doesn't take long banging your head against a wall before you say 'Hey, this hurts. I don't think I want to do this any more.'"

Losses in court produce frustration and are perceived of as insults by police who bring the cases to the prosecutor's office. I sometimes wondered how much of that frustration ended up being vented on suspected gang members when police encountered them on the street. On the other hand, interviews with prosecutors revealed their concern about gangs and frustration over understaffing in their offices and the sometimes incomplete or inadequately prepared cases brought to them by police. It takes a lot of work to build a solid case against a gang member - having to prove or show an act was gang-motivated or gang-related can be very difficult. The related legislation is a bit complicated.

Police Perceptions of Criminal Law, 
Judges, and Criminal Courts

Among most of the police I interviewed, judges faired poorly in terms of their knowledge of the gang situation in the community and their willingness to act decisively in court with convicted gang members.

Field Note: A gang unit officer said "I caught a shoot (a murder case), and because of the courts he was out on bail the next day. The courts are just too lenient. I don't think the judges really know what's going on." Later in the day I asked if the gang unit had ever taken a judge out for a ride-along. "No. Would it really help?"
Field Note: The two officers I rode with today were in court yesterday from eleven in the morning to four in the afternoon. They had worked from six until two in the morning and are expected to be in court tomorrow morning at eight. "The only reason we have to be in court tomorrow is because the defense attorney is inexperienced. He keeps making mistakes and asking for continuances [delays]."

Many of the gang unit officers saw the court as their enemy. This sentiment was shared by police in other units and divisions. Their perception was that judges undermine the work of police and, in effect, cause more crime to occur because offenders are not properly punished. They also resented the manner in which they were treated by the judge (i.e., being called to testify and waiting for hours only to find that the judge knew the case had been delayed but didn't bother to notify the testifying officer).

Field Note: The two gang unit officers I rode with today worked several months on a gang-related case. "We were in court for three days. The verdict came back in three hours. He [the gang member] was sentenced to three days in jail. With time served, he was out on the street that day."

The perception (real or not) that judges are too lenient and that sending gang-member-suspects through the system is futile has resulted in police meting out street justice. That is, suspects are dealt with (punished) on the street rather than through the courts. Disputes are overlooked, settled on the street, or the suspects are brought into the police station, questioned, held in lock-up for a while (as their informal punishment/treatment), then released. For some police this is a last ditch effort in hopes of resolving the problems gang members present short of making an arrest and attempting to get the case into court.

Police Perceptions of 
Juvenile Law and Juvenile Courts

Field Note: The female gang unit officer I interviewed, who's been in the unit for nearly eight years, said "The juvenile court is a joke!" 

Police concern about juveniles, juvenile law, and juvenile courts is understandable. Involvement in gangs often begins at an early age. It is not uncommon to find gang-member wannabes who are just eight or nine years old. The situation with juveniles today is different than it was forty or fifty years ago when much of the juvenile-related legislation was written.

Field Note: A gang unit supervisor said "The state's Criminal Code for juveniles was written in the 1950's with a mind set of Parens Patriae. But, for law enforcement, we get resistance from the juvenile justice system. Frankly, the difference between an act of delinquency in the 1950's and an act of delinquency in the 1990's are so different that they are incomparable. But the laws remain the same."

Field Note: Another officer believes "Youthful offenders are committing very serious crimes today and, because of the laws and the courts, we respond by just slapping them on the hand and treating them as children. These kids are not naive. They know how serious their criminality is. They know the consequences of their actions as far as they effect their victims."

Until 1899 most juveniles in the United States were treated as adults if they broke the law. As long as the child was over 9 or 10 years of age, he or she was regarded as an adult. That's a left over from our days as a British Colony. In 1899, however, Cook County (Chicago), Illinois, created the first juvenile court and defined youths 9 to 16 years of age as "juveniles" or "minors." They were not to be treated as adults if they broke the law.

As noted previously, a new philosophy was created for the handling of juvenile offenders. In short, justice for juveniles was to take place in court privately, using informal procedures, and should result in efforts to reform or rehabilitate youthful offenders. The criminal (adult) justice process, on the other hand, was to be public, formal, and punitive. These differences in philosophy as they are applied to juvenile gang members do not sit well with some police. 

Field Note: A long-time gang unit supervisor in Colorado said "Outside the department is a major problem. As it is now, the current political environment is so behind the reality of the gang problem on the street. Even after Columbine. Lots of legislators and practitioners in the juvenile justice system in our state say that our Juvenile Code is the best possible. In reality, it's an encumbrance to the police and the gang unit. It views young offenders as children and it's too concerned with their welfare."

Field Note: A gang unit officer in California believes "An example of the softness of the juvenile law is that the juvenile courts are too lenient. We arrest the kid on Saturday night and he's back on the street Monday morning."

Field Note: A gang unit officer, previously a juvenile officer, said "Our juvenile justice system is doing nothing, or making things worse because there are no consequences for these children. Children who violate the law are not dealt with harshly enough by the juvenile courts."

Paperwork and Case Development

Urban policing today involves a great deal of report writing. Patrol officers are dispatched to an incident, study the crime scene, question witnesses, collect testimony, and fill out reports carefully noting everything he or she observed and was told. While at the scene, police are likely to receive another call from the dispatcher which beckons them to the next incident where, once again, they study the crime scene, question witnesses at the scene, collect testimony, fill out a report, and so it goes incident after incident, day after day. At the end of each day, the officer is usually expected to rewrite the reports, making sure they are correct in every detail, often having to input them into a computer.

On the other hand, gang unit officers, and officers in other specialized units (i.e., homicide, rape, burglary, narcotics) may have an opportunity to work a case from beginning (responding to a call for service like a patrol officer does) to end (conducting the investigation and taking the case to the prosecutor). 

Officers in specialized units may even choose which cases to pursue and which to drop based on certain screening criteria (i.e., the amount or quality of evidence available, the testimony of credible witnesses, the seriousness of the alleged offense, the nature of the offender, the insistence of the victim, the political impact of the incident). When asked what they liked most about their work, gang unit officers often replied "I like the autonomy - choosing the cases I want to make and taking them from start to finish."

The perceptions gang unit officers, and others, develop are often the result of various difficulties they have encountered in their work. Those difficulties are out next topic of discussion.
