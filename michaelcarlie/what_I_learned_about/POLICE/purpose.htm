The Purpose of a Gang Unit

Some police officers assigned to the gang problem have directly provided counseling, job development and referral, and tutoring, and have engaged in extensive community relations and development activities.  

In some cities where these more complex approaches have been tried, some evidence shows a decline in the youth gang problem. But ... it is not clear whether the decline was due to changed police strategies or alternate but unrelated structural changes in the community environment, such as more legitimate jobs becoming available or greater access to income producing drug trafficking. (Spergel, et al., 1994, p. 8)

Prior to my field observations I thought the sole purpose of a police gang unit was to arrest gang members. I was wrong.

In Closing

The Goal of a Police Gang Unit

Among all the questions I asked police gang unit members, few received as many conflicting replies as the question "What's the purpose or goal of your gang unit?" I found members of the same gang unit with conflicting goals, the goals of gang units poorly articulated throughout the department in which they were located, and, across departments, gang units with differing goals.

Field Note: I asked the Lieutenant who supervised the city's gang unit what he established as the unit's goals. "The first goal is that my gang unit officers treat other people as they would wish to be treated themselves. Second, my officers should understand why gang members are doing what they are doing and third, the officers should be productive." He defined productive as "coming off duty being able to say 'We or I did a good job today.'" He said productivity is "not measured by the number of arrests made or miles driven." Finally, he said, "I want my officers to get along well with their fellow workers."

Later in the evening I asked the Sergeant of the gang unit the same question ("What are the goals of the gang unit?"). He said "Making arrests, seizing drugs, property, and guns, collecting evidence useful in obtaining convictions and doing all of this with a respect for due process."

What I found most confusing was observing gang units which had responsibilities for other kinds of offenses or offenders. A case in point was one gang unit which was responsible for vice crimes (i.e., gambling, prostitution, and other sex offenses), organized crime, and gangs. The offenders/offenses upon which the unit focused depended upon the whims of the chief.  His choices depended upon directions given him by the City Manager who, in turn, was being responsive to public pressure.

My own feeling was that multi-tasking a gang unit weakened efforts in all of its endeavors. They worked gangs, started building needed intelligence, then were taken away from that work and placed on vice crimes. Once the vice offenders had been dealt with the officers returned to work gangs but the intelligence was no longer current.

In most of the departments I visited the gang unit was just that - all they dealt with was gangs. My task was to determine what the goals of the gang units were and how they attempted to reach them. In one community I asked a gang unit member what his primary concern was. He replied "I am concerned for the safety and quality of life of people in this community. Making arrests is secondary to developing a high quality of life for them."

In another community I asked a seasoned gang unit officer the same question. He said "The goals of this gang unit are suppression and wiping these guys out. There's no changing the ones who are committed to the life. They don't know anything else and they won't listen to anything else."

I learned that the primary goal of any police gang unit is to produce a reduction in gang activity. While it make take the entire community to produce this result, the police are expected to make their contribution towards that goal. At the procedural level, the goal of all gang units is to carry out their activities with respect for the due process rights of everyone they encounter. Without due process, few cases will be won in court unless, of course, one relies upon plea bargaining as a consequence of a poorly managed case.

In their research, Katz and Webb found "wide variations in the existence and adequacy of written polices and procedures governing the gang units that we studied." One gang unit had no written guidelines and no formal documentation of the unit's functions. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  184)  On the other hand, the researchers found a gang unit with a continually updated, fifty-page document outlining, in some detail, the standard operating procedures of that unit. Without clearly articulated and agreed-to policies and procedures, the gang unit takes on the culture of the department or, at the least, the culture of the gang unit passed along from one member to another.

In their report, Police Response to Gang: A Multi-Site Study, Katz and Webb found that

In addition to providing gang unit officers with guidance as to their functions and the activities that they were to perform, the Las Vegas unit's policy manual described general issues pertaining to the coordination of training, the operation of the section's library, the management of criminal intelligence information, and records retention. It also provided information pertaining to officer uniforms, property assigned to officers, personal appearance, and the issuance of departmental vehicles. The manual dedicated considerable space to specific procedures for disseminating gang intelligence, handling confidential informants, maintaining investigative files, making media releases, establishing seizure logs, and obtaining search warrants. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  189)

The Duties of a Police Gang Unit

Where they exist, gang units vary in complexity from simple one-person operations having one function (typically gathering intelligence for all the department's other units) to multi-group, multi-layered organizations collecting intelligence, carrying out investigations, participating in multi-agency task forces and with sub-groups each dedicated to the pursuit of one type or kind of gang or another.

Generalizing from my observations and those from Katz's and Webb's experience in Las Vegas, Phoenix, Albuquerque, and Inglewood (CA) (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  172), the primary duties of a police gang unit include gathering intelligence, carrying out investigations, applying suppression/enforcement, offering education (for prevention and intervention purposes), and involvement in a gang task force.

The intelligence function, which, as Klein found, was the primary function of 83% of gang units across the United States (Klein, 1995), was discussed previously. Carrying out investigations is part and parcel of building intelligence and is often used to justify requests for search warrants and seizure/arrest warrants.

[In their research on the Las Vegas, Albuquerque, Inglewood (CA), and Phoenix Police Departments' gang units, Katz and Webb found that] "... some of the gang units that we studied were devoting relatively little time to criminal investigation activities. The gang units' involvement in criminal investigation tended to be indirect, performed largely as part of the intelligence function."  (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  176)

Another aspect of the intelligence function is that it provides other members of the police department with needed information. Katz and Webb relate a comment made to them during their research by an Inglewood (CA) gang unit officer. Asked why other, non-gang-unit officers turned to him for information, he replied

Probably the knowledge of knowing almost everybody on the street, because our job is to contact and talk and become personally involved in these gangsters' lives, their family lives, their mothers, their school lives, so we get to know the people. We don't know them all, I mean, there is just too many of them to know, but we know most of them, the ones that are the most active, and we know a lot about them, and they talk to us, and we know a lot of what is going on in the street. And so we are kind of like an encyclopedia here of what is going on in the city, and everybody come to us because of it ... (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  303)

Suppression refers to deterring gang members from committing crimes by arresting them. Deterrence is further offered by educating the citizenry about gangs in hopes of preventing gang activity and gang joining, and intervening in the lives of those who are on the fringes of gang activity and membership. That educational process may take place through a community-wide task force on gangs and/or youth violence. A variety of methods are available for each of these primary duties. Unfortunately, like Katz and Webb (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  176), I found most of the gang units observed had little, if anything, to do with gang-related prevention efforts in their respective communities (save for some units being involved in the G.R.E.A.T. program).

A policy of suppression may also have consequences on the quantity and quality of intelligence a gang unit may collect. Katz and Webb report that, when asked why other police agencies contacted the Inglewood (CA) police gang unit for intelligence instead of the Los Angeles Police Department's gang unit, one Inglewood officer said

LAPD CRASH (Los Angles Police Department's gang unit) can not get fuck for intelligence. All they do is enforcement. They bust heads, so nobody will talk to them ... (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  304)

The inner-workings of a gang unit are designed around the primary duties. In some units there are one or more officers assigned to each - intelligence officers, investigators, and those who make arrests. The intelligence officers and investigators may each be assigned a different kind of gang to observe (i.e., Asian gangs, Hispanic gangs, African-American gangs, Bikers, Gangster Disciples, Bloods, Skinheads). In other units officers may share all of these responsibilities.

In addition to intelligence, investigation, and suppression, other functions or duties of a police gang unit may include:

parental notification (notifying parents of their child's gang involvement),

meeting and conversing with families in their homes,

presenting cases to the prosecutor,

testifying in court,

providing education programs in the police academy,

educating the public and students (i.e., through implementation of the Gang Resistance Education and Training [G.R.E.A.T.] program [see under "Next" at the bottom of this page], auditorium presentations, classroom discussions),

participating in police task force units (combined efforts with federal, state, county, and other municipal police agencies),

participating on task force groups consisting of non-police members (i.e., concerned citizens, probation/parole, juvenile services, members of the faith community, school administrators, educators, representatives from area neighborhood associations),

working with graffiti removal services,

assisting School Resource Officers,

working with schools, faith community, business community and other local social institutions to provide information, gather information and offer leadership, and

communicating with the media.

Of the four police department gang units they studied, Katz and Webb state that the Las Vegas unit was the only one with clearly articulated goals and objectives. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  198)  Without goals and objectives, what do officers or supervisors do when they join and work in such a unit and how do the department, the media, or the community assess their performance? Katz and Webb report the goals and objectives of the Las Vegas (NV) Police Department's gang unit as follows.

The Gang Investigation Section (GIS) of the Organized Crime Bureau (OCB) of the Special Operations Division (SOD) will be responsible for the lawful collection, analyzation (sic), dissemination of intelligence information, and the follow-up investigation of gang-motivated crime, except crimes involving homicide, sexual assault, or high profile takeover robberies, such as casino robberies.

The objectives of the Gang Investigation Section are to suppress street gang criminal activity through lawful arrests and prosecution, and to deter street gang criminal activity through the lawful collection, analyzation (sic), and dissemination of intelligence information. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  198)

In addition to identifying suppression, investigation, and education/prevention as describing how gang unit officers allocate their time, Katz and Webb also noted some gang unit officers also allocated time to administrative duties, en route activity, and non-police related functions as follows.

(Administrative activities included) Field preparation, report production, evidence processing, meeting with court officials, testifying in court, and job training. (En route activity included) [t]ravel to and from official destinations while (non-police related activity included) [a]ctivities unrelated to official police business, e.g., meals and snacks, personal errands, restroom and other breaks. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  259)

Methods of Policing Gangs

Police have a variety of methods and tactics at their disposal for reducing gang activity in a community. In addition to gathering and analyzing intelligence and making arrests (suppression), other methods used include deterrence, education, mediation, and diversion and referral. Each of these are explored below while tactics are addressed later in the book.

Deterrence

Deterrence is achieved by police gang units in a variety of ways. The goal of deterrence is to keep gang activity from occurring or to reduce the amount of gang activity taking place. "The underlying assumption of the suppression strategy is based on deterrence theory: that swift, severe, and certain punishment will lead to the reduction of gang-related activity among current gang members (also referred to as specific deterrence), as well as to a deduction in the number of individuals who want to participate in gangs and gang behavior in the future (referred to as general deterrence)." (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  15, italics added by Carlie.)

The most typical method used is maintaining a physical presence in the neighborhood. Among the techniques used to do this are:

a. Focused routine preventive patrol: Focused routine preventive patrol finds police driving marked or unmarked police cars through gang territory using a random pattern. While unmarked cars are recognized as easily as marked cars, it is hoped that the continued but irregular appearance of either will deter gang activity.

b. Directed patrol: Directed patrol results from the police driving marked and unmarked cars through gang territory in a pre-determined manner. One example of this technique is when three police cars drive in tandem down each of three adjacent and parallel streets. The objective of this technique is to find individuals who may be avoiding the police by "squeezing" them down the streets to a predetermined place where another police car awaits.  Much like squeezing a tube of toothpaste, regardless of where the toothpaste is in the tube, upon squeezing it the toothpaste comes one at only one place. That's where a police car awaits the fleeing gang member.

c. Non-vehicular patrol: For lack of a better name, non-vehicular patrol refers to the employment of anything other than a police car while using the techniques described above. In this regard, police may use bicycles, horses, motorcycles, helicopters, and traditional foot patrol to deter gang activity.

The following is an account of a typical "stop" conducted by gang unit officers.

When an individual who had been stopped did fit the profile of gang membership, the officer would assess whether he or she was actually involved in gangs. First, officers would order the suspect out of the car and ask for a driver's license, vehicle registration, and insurance documents. Meanwhile, the officers were scanning the suspect's arms, hands, and ankles for gang tattoos, and examining their clothing, belt buckle, shoes, and hair style for indications of involvement in the gang lifestyle.

Officers also examined personal property such as school books or bags for gang graffiti, and interviewed the suspect about gang affiliation. The officer would ask the suspect questions: Where are you going? Who are you down with? Who do you hang out with? Were you ever down with a gang? All of this would take place while the officer's partner was running the individual's name for possible warrants.

If the officer determined that the individual was a gang member or met the criteria for gang membership, he completed a FI (field interview) card and took a Polaroid picture of the person. He would also ask permission to search the vehicle. Even though typically no reason was given for searches, all suspects whom we observed permitted the officers to search their vehicles. If the individual had no warrants for arrest and if there was no incriminating evidence, he or she would be released. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  275)

The next most common method of deterring gang activity is to arrest gang members. Sometimes the arrests are made to simply take someone (or several people) off the street for a day or two and sometimes they are made in hopes of getting a conviction and removing the suspect for a longer period of time.

Education

In Schools:

Gang unit personnel, much like D.A.R.E. (Drug Awareness and Resistance Education) officers, attempt to deter gang membership and gang activity through a variety of education programs. These programs include making presentations to students, teachers, security personnel, and administrators in the local schools. 

Audiences are informed about a number of things including the nature and danger of gangs, how to determine if a student is involved in gang activity, who to report gang activity to, how to secure the school against gang activity, and what role the school can play in helping its students and staff avoid gang involvement. The United States Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, and Firearms (ATF) has, in conjunction with the Phoenix (Arizona) Police Department, developed a course of instruction called the Gang Resistance Education and Training  (G.R.E.A.T.) to accomplish this goal. 

Through a series of nine one-hour in-class sessions, gang unit officers provide 3rd and 5th grade students an opportunity to develop a means of resisting gangs. The program is built upon the belief that youths who have a positive self-concept, accept and embrace diversity, and develop non-violent techniques for resolving conflict are less likely to be interested in joining a gang.

For the Business and Faith Communities:

Similar education efforts are offered members of the business community (through presentations to such organizations as the Chamber of Commerce, Kiwanis, Lions, Rotary, and Junior League) and faith community. Health care providers, the media, and other social institutions may also be included.

For the General Public:

Appearances on local television and radio programs are another aspect of the public education function of a police gang unit. Joining discussion and debate panels, making individual appearances, and offering insights on the news of the day as it relates to gangs are some of the public education functions of the unit.

Publications prepared by the gang unit may also be created and circulated throughout the community and web sites are sometimes created which can be used to further educate the user about gangs.

Community-Wide Task Force involvement:

In communities throughout the United States, police gang unit personnel participate in local task force groups in an effort to prevent and/or reduce gang activity and to educate the public. A typical task force consists of representatives from many of the community's social institutions including the faith institutions, schools, mental health agencies, juvenile and family services, representatives from neighborhood associations, business community, law enforcement, the prosecutor's office, and other concerned citizens.

Mediation

I observed gang unit officers mediating conflicts and potential conflicts between gang members both within the same gang and between rival gang members. Although in all instances the mediation was impromptu, it was mediation all the same.

A conflict between two gang members was happening or about to happen and the officer entered the situation and attempted to get the parties to resolve the conflict without the use of violence. Ex-gang members have been known to be involved in such efforts in the past, but the involvement of police in mediation remains a relatively unexplored topic. 

Diversion and Referral

In general, diversion and referral is a process of diverting alleged offenders from the juvenile- or criminal justice system in order to refer them to a community agency, organization, or program so they can get help.

This process is used by gang unit officers when they attempt to steer a youth involved in gang activity to a community agency for help. It is most often used with offenders who have committed minor crimes and those who are not yet committed to the gang life. Another form of referral and diversion occurs when gang unit officers provide parental notification.

Parental Notification:

Depending upon the community, police gang unit officers or truant officers may be used to notify parents of the school's belief that their child is involved in a gang or gang behavior. Typically received in the mail, the notification explains the basis for the school's belief and invites the parents and their child to meet with school representatives trained to discuss the matter. Through this technique it is hoped that early detection and family-oriented action, in conjunction with the school, will deter youths from further gang involvement.

Home Visits:

Gang unit officers, at the request of concerned parents or guardians, will sometimes visit a home in order to talk with a child who is believed (by the parents or guardians) to be involved with gang youth or in gang behavior. The methods used to achieve this end include the use of threats ("If I'm called out here again to talk with you, you can bet I'll be watching for you on the street."), cautions ("If you keep on acting like this you're going to end up with these guys and your future will be ruined."), and counseling ("We have some people [or a program, etc.] who would be willing to help you - if you want someone to talk to or help you with school work. Would you like to me to have them call you?").   

Field Note: I rode with the gang unit supervisor again today. He received a call from a concerned parent. She wanted the supervisor to come to her home and speak with her 17 year old son about his behavior. The supervisor knew the young man as a gang wannabe from past experiences on the street.

We arrived at the home and the supervisor spoke with both the mother and father for nearly 30 minutes before the son came home. The supervisor's attention then turned to the son as he asked him how he was doing. In the process he asked the young man to enter his bedroom and, while there, the supervisor looked over the room, opened dresser drawers, leafed through papers at the head of the bed, and asked a lot of questions.

The conversation turned to cautions as the supervisor warned the young man of the possible consequences of his actions. After talking with him for nearly an hour, we left the home. About four months later I received an email from the supervisor. He shared with me a conversation he recently had with the mother of the boy and learned that he had enlisted in the marines. This was a positive ending to a potentially sad story. 

Given the myriad and time-consuming activities in which gang unit officers may be involved, it is not surprising to find that "gang unit officers had less contact with gang members than many might expect." (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  311) 

A Note on the Management of a Police Gang Unit

I asked the commanders of several gang units what the most difficult or challenging aspects of managing a gang unit were. They identified the following most often:

The gang unit must have a well defined purpose (provide prevention and/or intervention, gather intelligence, investigate, use suppression, or do some combination of them all).

That purpose must be supported throughout upper administration.

There must be a shared perception and useful definition of what the local gang situation is throughout the unit and the rest of the department. This includes an acceptable and widely-shared definition of a gang and a gang member.

There must be well defined and well used lines of communication between the various relevant units within the police department (i.e., patrol, juvenile, robbery, homicide, narcotics, property crime) and between the gang unit and probation/parole officers, juvenile officers, the community, and other resources.

After riding with a dozen or so different department's gang units, it became obvious that there was a poor fit between what supervisors thought the gang unit should be doing and what the gang unit officers thought they were supposed to be doing. Katz and Webb also found this to be the case when they stated that

Standing operating procedures were absent or underdeveloped. The (gang) units lacked formal goals, objectives, benchmarks, and performance measures that could be used to gauge their effectiveness. Administrative oversight, formal organizational control, and managerial direction for the gang units was largely absent, and unit supervision was minimal. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  218)

Measuring the Effectiveness of a Gang Unit

How does one go about measuring whether the work done by gang unit officers is effective? What does "effective" mean? Katz and Webb identify a number of performance criteria as found in the departments they studied. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  201-205) Among the most common measures identified, many of them suppression/intelligence-oriented, were:

tracking the numbers of gang members,
 
the number of reported drive-by-shootings,
 
the number deceased gang members,
 
the number of cases cleared,
 
the number of arrests (by type),
 
the number and size of seizures,
 
the number of gang-related cases initiated and assisted,
 
the number of weapons and narcotics recovered,
 
the number of field interviews (a primary source of intelligence),
 
the amount of gang and non-gang crime,
 
number of gang-related cases submitted for prosecution,
 
the amount of reported graffiti,
 
the amount of drugs taken off the streets by gang-unit officers,
 
the number of individuals documented as gang members,
 
the number of requests received for gang-related intelligence,
 
the amount of time spent responding to such requests,
 
number of citations issued,
 
citizens' perception of their safety and quality of life, and
 
whether or not the city council was satisfied with the gang unit's efforts.

The emphasis placed upon suppression is understandable. It is often "... a consequence of community, political, and media demands." (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  217) Serious offenders must be removed from the community.

One of the more significant findings of Katz's and Webb's research was the fact that "...none of the gang units collected intelligence on gangs, per se. Identification of a particular gang's members always preceded documentation of the gang. The bottom line is that, with the exception of (one of the four department's studied), none of the gang units ... gathered intelligence directly and proactively on gangs as groups." (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  283, italics added for emphasis.) This is a flaw if a gang unit hopes to develop accurate, complete, and useable intelligence.

But what of the other functions of a gang unit - prevention and/or intervention? What are the performance measures for those duties of a gang unit? Perhaps we should consider the:

number of hours of public presentations on gangs by gang unit members (education),
 
number of residents attending those presentations,
 
number of memberships on boards of citizens working on the problems that cause gang formation and the
 
number of community-based meetings attended by members of the gang unit (attending community task force meetings, drug-abuse prevention board meetings, etc.),
 
number of hours volunteering for community service related to the problems that cause gang formation,
 
proportion of gang unit officers able to speak the native language of the community's gang population (i.e., Spanish, Korean, etc.), and
 
the racial/ethnic composition of the gang unit (does it reflect the composition of the community it serves?).

At the end of the day, no gang unit, no matter how well trained, how large, or how effective, is going to rid a community of its gangs. Logic suggests that, if a gang unit were successful in its efforts, the number of gang-related arrests would increase, more cases would be submitted for prosecution, and the cases would have sufficient prosecutorial merit to conclude with a conviction and long prison term (and enhanced whenever possible).

Such a situation will result in more gang members being sent to prison and, almost certainly, to a long-term increase in the number of gang members and the number of gang-related/motivated crimes. Prison does not cure criminality. On the contrary, time in prison makes it practically impossible for any inmate to build a law-abiding life once released into the community (few employers are interested in hiring them). And survival in prison often requires joining a gang for self-protection.

Just think of the meaning of these statistics: about 90% of all prisoners in the United States will eventually be release or discharged from prison. Every year, nearly 500,000 inmates are being released/discharged back into society. A percentage of those inmates are gang members. They were gang members before they arrived in prison or became gang members in prison. The flow of gang members into and out of America's prisons is well documented and suggests that a suppression-only policy will fail to effectively reduce the number of gang members, the number of gangs, or the amount of crime committed by gang members.

A suppression-only policy is likely doomed. Only when suppression is coupled with prevention (keeping youth from joining gangs) and intervention (helping those loosely associated with a gang leave the gang) will the dynamic work. And the police are not responsible for all three approaches. The entire community must enter the fray. Every individual is responsible as are the faith community, the business community, and the local educational system.

Katz and Webb offer the following as reasons why evaluating gang units is so important.

Without performance measures, managers are unable to make effective administrative decisions relating to training, officer evaluations, and promotions. Performance measures allow managers to provide feedback and guidance to unit personnel, so they can continue to grow in productivity and effectiveness. Systematic evaluation of the unit and its personnel provides information to managers concerning the means that the unit uses to address gang-related problems. It keeps management up-to-date on the support (i.e., personnel and other resources) needed to address the gang problem.

Performance evaluations provide a means of formally socializing gang unit officers and holding them accountable. The measures convey agency expectations and inform unit officers, in an official and formal way, about the mission, goals, and priorities of the unit. Performance measures are essentially a detailed list of expectations regarding the types and numbers of activities that are to be performed and their quality. Performance evaluations also socialize officers informally, communicating acceptable styles of policing, and they help to create a shared vision of what constitutes successful gang control. Finally, performance measure facilitate professional development among officers in the unit. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p. 455)

Who Should Serve in a Police Gang Unit?

What type of officers should be in a police department's gang unit? If the primary goal is suppression (making arrests), that kind of gang unit would require a different kind of officer than would be required by a gang unit that is primarily intelligence and/or prevention-oriented. Katz and Webb found that, in the four department's they studied, gang unit officers had to have at least three (3) years of street experience as a cop. In addition they could not have a complaint record and had to be self-motivated. Previous experience with gangs or the gang unit was preferred as was the ability to speak one or more of the languages spoken by members of the local gangs and to be a member of one of the minority ethnic/racial groups found in the community. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  222-226)

In three of the four department's they studied, Katz and Webb note that "... they sought officers who would not require a great deal of oversight and who would be aggressive in the field." (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  223) "Perhaps the most interesting finding, however, turned out to be a qualification that was missing from the lists of gang unit managers" in three of the four cities they studied. "We were surprised to find that new gang unit officers were not required to have even minimal prior investigative experience, given the seriousness of the crimes that they would be required to investigate. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  227 italics added for emphasis.)

Police work is, to a very large extent, a solitary pursuit. Officers on foot patrol walk alone. Patrol officers drive around town alone in their cars hour after hour, day after day, year after year. When they happen upon a crime scene, they are typically alone, as they are when they prepare their reports. On the other hand, working in a police gang unit typically requires a mind-set that is compatible with working as a team member. As Katz and Webb found,

For some officers, the primary problems encountered after transferring to the gang unit were associated with the new working environment. As a group, these officers tended to identify three challenges. First, a number o them had difficulty adjusting to working as part of a team.

As patrol officers, many had worked specific heats and precincts, and had become intimately familiar with the community and streets. Once in the gang unit, they had to know the entire city, or at least those areas where the gang unit frequently worked. (Finally), [s]ome officers found working with gang members, their families, and friends to be more challenging than expected. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  244)

In Closing

Gang units may have conflicting or poorly articulated goals, but most have a common purpose - to conduct investigations, develop intelligence, and use the intelligence to address the local gang situation. While all the police gang units I studied used suppression (arrest) as their primary method of dealing with gangs, some used deterrence, education, mediation, and/or diversion and referral.

How are officers trained to accomplish the many duties and tasks assigned a gang unit? That's the next topic for consideration.
