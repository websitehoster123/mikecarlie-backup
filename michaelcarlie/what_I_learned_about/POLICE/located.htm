Part 2:
Where is the Gang Unit Located?

"The specific configuration and location of a unit within its parent organization might affect the organizational perception of the nature of the problem, and may also shape the specific programs and practices of the unit." (Katz and Webb, 2004, p. 27)

The way in which police departments organize to address a community's gang situation varies considerably. Some departments don't organize at all. Officers in patrol, narcotics, juvenile, and other units may stumble upon gang members or gang intelligence and may or may not share it for a variety of reasons.

What is a "gang unit?" According to Katz, McGuire, and Roncek (Katz, et. al. 2002), "a police gang unit is a secondary or tertiary functional division within the parent police organization, with at least one sworn officer whose sole function is to engage in gang control efforts."

Some departments assign an officer to work gangs while, in other departments, an officer volunteers to work gangs because of his or her own personal interests. There are departments with one gang unit and officers who are spread across the various shifts through the day and night. And there are departments with an entire division dedicated to working gangs. Within that division there will likely be individual units assigned to deal with Hispanic, African-American, Asian, or other ethnic gangs depending upon the make-up of the local gang population. And each unit may have three different shifts of officers.

It should not be surprising that police departments have chosen to create specialized gang units to focus, consolidate, and coordinate their gang control efforts, since a number of reasons have been offered to suggest that specialization is an effective and efficient means of organizing resources. However, not all of the gang units that we studied organized their units in the same way. Specially, their administrative locations, organizational configurations, functions, and management structures varied considerably from one department to the next. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  167)

If there is a police gang unit, where is it located in the police department's structure? This is an important question and the answer to it reveals a great deal about the department's administrators, their outlook on their community's gang situation, and more. Is the gang unit independent - unattached to any other division or unit in the department? Or is it attached to the narcotics unit, juvenile division, Special Operations Division (as in the Las Vegas Police Department), the Special Enforcement Bureau (as in the Albuquerque Police Department), or the Organized Crime Bureau (as in the Phoenix Police Department)?  The answer to this question reveals how the gang problem is being defined by the police administrator, the likely response of the police department to the community's gangs, and the public's perception of its local gangs and their activities.

It is popular today to associate gangs with drugs. To this, Malcolm Klein, with over 30 years of gang research under his belt, wrote that when the crack epidemic hit L.A. in the early 1980s,

The police and press version was that street gangs were ideal mechanisms for drug distribution. Gangs, it was said, were tightly organized, with a well-defined leadership, fierce internal loyalty, and well-established territories that they defended through violence. This gang image fit the needs of drug, or crack, distribution very neatly. Except that this is not what most street gangs are like. (Klein, 1995, pp. 16-17).

Regardless of the extent of gang-related drug crimes, placing a police gang unit inside a narcotics unit defines the gang problem as a drug problem. Because the anti-gang effort is placed in the narcotics unit, drug laws become the doorway to gangs and gang members will be arrested for drug law violations. In turn, through press releases and newspaper articles, the community will come to know its community's gang issue as a drug issue.

On the other hand, if the police gang unit is aligned with the department's juvenile division, gang members will be detained (juveniles are detained, adults are arrested - it's the same consequence, just called by a different name) for violations of delinquency laws. In this community, residents will come to know the gang issue as a juvenile delinquency issue.  

The orientation of the gang unit may also be a result of where the unit is located in the department's structure. My experience suggests gang units attached to narcotics units are more punitive and reactive in their approach to gang members than are units attached to the juvenile division. Police gang units associated with a juvenile division are more proactive and treatment oriented in their approach. This is understandable given the original purpose for the creation of the American juvenile justice system.

Sidebar: 
Differences in Philosophy between the American Juvenile Justice System and the Criminal Justice System

The American juvenile justice system is an outgrowth of the first juvenile court which was created in Cook County (Chicago), Illinois, in 1899. As the philosophy of the American juvenile justice system took shape it was determined that, whereas the criminal (adult) justice system demanded all proceedings be public, juvenile proceedings were to be held in private. It was believed the stigmatization and labeling resulting from holding proceedings in public would hinder a youth's ability to overcome his or her deviance.

Whereas the justice process for adults was formal (i.e., trials, verdicts), the process for juveniles was to be informal (i.e., hearings - not trials, findings - not verdicts, no jury). Being informal allowed the court/judge to respond to a deviant youth with compassion and understanding. The court could be more flexible in its response and would be concerned more with the "welfare of the child" than with simply imposing punishment.

This brings us to the third major difference between the criminal and juvenile justice systems. The criminal (adult) justice system was based upon the philosophy that adults knew what they were doing when they violated the law and needed to be punished in order for their behavior to be altered. The juvenile justice system is based upon the belief that young people can be rehabilitated without resorting to punishment. They needed treatment. 

The association of a police gang unit with the police juvenile division, then, is more likely to exhibit a treatment orientation.

Placement of a police gang unit in the narcotics division, on the other hand, suggests a punitive approach. In departments where the gang unit stands alone, the orientation is more likely to be varied and may depend upon the wishes of the unit's commander, the officers which serve on the unit, the community's wishes, the influence of local politics, or a combination of all these forces and others.

As related to intelligence, I noticed that gang units associated with narcotics were less likely to have good intelligence on gang members who were minors (not adults). Gang units associated with juvenile units, on the other hand, had intelligence on the minors. This is an important distinction since intelligence on minors in gangs means police may be able to identify youths who can be helped before they become too involved with a gang.

Where is the gang unit located in the community? Some police gang units work out of the department's headquarters and/or substations. Others are off-site, located in the community but with addresses unknown to the public. Some move from time to time to keep their location secret.

Referring to three of the four police department gang units they studied, Katz and Webb noted that "The locations were kept secret, and as a result, were unavailable to the public, community leaders, and other criminal justice officials." (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  214) My own inclination is to be critical of this latter policy because it further separates the community (which, in the end, is the body that is going to have to address the underlying causes for gang formation and gang joining) from the police gang unit officers. For intelligence to flow from gang situation to police to community, there must be contact and communication. Off-site locations hinder such communication.

Compounding the exclusionary effect of having a gang unit located off-site is further separation of the gang unit from the department due to the individual gang unit officer's  autonomy. Katz and Webb found that "Some gang unit officers commented that they enjoyed special status within the department because of the many benefits they received while in the (gang) unit. For example, some noted, no supervisors looked over their shoulders; they were self-directed in the field; (and they worked in individual cubicles." (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  229)

We've been looking at where the gang unit may be located in a department or the community and what the consequences of those locations are. Now we'll take a look at the purpose of a gang unit.
