Part 9:
When a Police Gang Unit "Crosses the Line"

Field Note: I interviewed a law enforcement officer who is well-respected in law enforcement circles and is known nationally for his expertise in the area of Latino gangs. I asked him about the relationship between police and gang members and he said "I am dismayed by the fact that there are police who deal harshly with gang members on the street even though, at that moment, they may have done nothing to deserve the anger being expressed. You don't have to hate these guys to work with them. In fact, if we could get rid of the hate that police feel, we might be more effective in working to rid the community of gangs."

By "crossing the line" I refer to gang unit officers who agitate gang members and exacerbate the relationship between police and gang members. Evidence of such behavior includes unprovoked yelling, name calling, use of insulting or derogatory language, verbal threats, pushing, hitting, and other assaults upon the person or reputation of a known or suspected gang member. In some cases, it includes criminal acts by the police.

Among the incidents of crossing the line I observed was one in which the gang unit officer, a female, carried a military-type knife that has a blade that is eight inches long and about two inches wide. She wore it strapped to the top of her right thigh. You might remember the story.

Field Note:  I told a gang unit supervisor that I had come to feel as though police sometimes make gang situations worse through their belligerence with suspects and innocent bystanders. "You're absolutely right," he said. "Once the police see that the prosecutor isn't going to take their gang cases seriously they sometimes get a little lazy and start to make mistakes - like conducting searches without probable cause, harassing suspects, writing insufficient or sloppy reports. 

"When police act the way you described we lose opportunities for cooperation with citizens and suspects. We lose intelligence. And it sets up a situation for the next officer who comes on the scene with a problem dealing with the suspects' and neighborhood's anger."

One of the situations I encountered involved a police gang unit with five officers. The supervisor of the unit, having learned the county prosecutor was not interested in prosecuting suspected gang members as members of a gang, began roughing up gang members on the street. He violated suspects' due process rights and assaulted them. The supervisor's activities were known to the prosecutor and, in time, the chief of the police department was told by the prosecutor he would have to bring the matter to court (by prosecuting the gang unit supervisor) if something wasn't done to correct the matter. "I was taken out of the gang unit the next day," the supervisor told me. "They transferred me to a desk job in another unit in the department." 

I spent several nights cruising with police gang unit officers in a West Coast American city. One night we saw four youths spread-eagled on a patrol car. They had been stopped by a patrol officer for a traffic violation and it happened to be in front of the apartment in which one of the youth's lived. That youth's father was at the scene along with several other family members and a few neighbors.

Field Note: A long-time gang member told me "The police act like they hate us ... even if we were doin' nothin'."

Two other gang unit officers arrived at the scene before we did and were standing near the crowd when we arrived. We pulled up, got out of the car, and approached the four boys. I heard the father asking one of the patrol officers "What are you bothering my son for? He's worked on getting done with his parole, he doesn't do drugs no more, he's going to school and doing well. Leave him alone. He's not one of the gang members."

Just then one of the gang unit officers yelled at the father "Then why's he running with these guys (pointing to the other three youths)!?" An extended and loud argument ensued between the gang unit officer and the father. Both were becoming angrier and more indignant by the second. "Where are you when we need you?", the father yelled. "We have kids sellin' drugs on the corner every night and here you are botherin' my son! You can't be a nigger here!" At this point another gang unit officer announced "We won't go there. That line of talk is something we will not do."

The patrol officer (not a member of the gang unit) who initiated pulling the car over in the first place, walked slowly from where the boys were into the shouting match and handed the father his business card quietly informing him "This is my personal phone number. When you see something we should know about, call me. Call me as soon as you can and as often as you need. OK?" The father stopped yelling, stared at the patrol officer and extended his hand for a handshake. Calmly, he thanked the patrol officer and stepped back. Calm had been restored.

No sooner did that happen than the gang unit officer began yelling at the father again. "You're not taking proper care of your son!" And the screaming started all over again. It seems to me some police create unpleasant situations themselves. I wondered if anyone was going to do something about the gang unit officer who was so aggressive and offensive. Later in the evening, without my saying a word, one of the gang unit officers I was riding with said "He must just be in a bad mood tonight," referring to the gang unit officer in question.

Field Note: One of the gang members I interviewed in prison told me "The worst thing a kid could see is a bad cop. When they see that, kids lose respect and support for the police. And the police represent everything that is supposed to be good and worthwhile. If that's the case - cops hurting people for no good reason - then the kids rebel and they do something stupid. The ones we really need to watch are the bad cops."

In 1999 a member of the Ramparts Division gang unit, Los Angeles Police Department, was caught stealing several pounds of cocaine from the department's property room. It had a reported street value of one million dollars. Caught by other police, he confessed and, in a plea bargain negotiated with the prosecutor, provided testimony against other officers in the gang unit. 

He told investigators about the activities of other gang unit members including the killing of innocent people, falsification of testimony in court, assaulting gang members, lying to officers and others outside the gang unit, and observance of a code of silence to stifle investigations into their colleagues' illegal behaviors.   

CRASH is the acronym for the Community Resources Against Street Hoodlums, an anti-gang program the (Los Angeles Police Department) implemented over a decade ago. The Rampart Division covers an eight square-mile area, just west of downtown, which is largely working class, heavily immigrant and densely populated.

LAPD officer Rafael Perez joined Rampart CRASH in 1996. In 1998 Perez was arrested for stealing eight pounds of cocaine, valued at a million dollars, from the Rampart evidence locker. In 1999 Perez began to cooperate in giving evidence against his former associates in the hope of receiving a reduced sentence on the cocaine theft charge. On February 24, 2000 Perez received a five-year prison term on the cocaine theft charges, but he is expected to stay in jail only a little over a year more, given time served and credit for good behavior.

Last month the Los Angeles Times published many of the revelations from 2000 pages of Perez's testimony. The law-breaking activity among police was so pervasive that the Times concluded: “An organized criminal subculture thrived within the LAPD, where a secret fraternity of anti-gang officers and supervisors committed crimes and celebrated shootings.”

Among the most chilling revelations concern outright police murders or attempted murders. In 1996 CRASH officer Kulin Patel shot Juan Saldana when he was running down an apartment hallway. Patel and his partner then planted a gun on Saldana after he went down. When the CRASH supervisor, Sergeant Edward Ortiz, arrived, he delayed calling an ambulance so the officers could concoct a cover story. Saldana bled to death by the time he arrived at the hospital.

In another incident CRASH officers fired 10 rounds at Carlos Vertiz, a 44-year-old man with no criminal record, after they mistook him for a drug dealer. To justify the shooting, officers then planted a shotgun near the dying Vertiz which they claimed he had pointed at them.

In 1996 Perez and his partner Nino Durden shot 19-year-old Javier Ovando in the chest and head and then planted a gun on him. Ovando received a draconian 23-year prison sentence because he would not show contrition. In fact, he was innocent. Ovando was released last year, after serving two years. As a result of his injuries he is now confined to a wheelchair.

On New Year's eve 1996, Rampart CRASH officers opened fire on and wounded two holiday revelers, afterwards arresting them on trumped-up charges. The officers then rehearsed the story that they had fired in self-defense, claiming the revelers had fired guns in the officers' direction. One unnamed officer has reportedly told his attorney that the CRASH cops were out “hunting” that night, that is, looking for people to ambush in sport.

Perez told investigators the lengths to which Rampart officers and their supervisors went to cover up bad shootings. In one instance, a rookie patrol officer shot a man when he opened a closet during a search and was startled to see the man inside. When the rookie's supervisor arrived at the scene he decided the rookie should say the man was holding a mirror, causing him to see his own reflection with a gun and open fire, thinking he had encountered an armed suspect. (Knowland and Nebbia, 2000, page)

Field Note: The gang unit Lieutenant told me that he tells his gang unit personnel "If you want to thump someone, do it before you get to the station." 

Added to the Rodney King case, incidents like Ramparts place a community's police in a perilous position. The public questions their professionalism, distrusts them, and, without the support of the community, police efforts to reduce gang activity (or any criminal activity, for that matter) are handicapped. 

In response to the Ramparts' gang unit's behavior, a former Los Angeles gang member said "Cops are just a gang with the law on their side." 

 

Field Note: I told the Lieutenant who presides over the gang unit that several of his gang unit officers were rude and aggressive with youths on the street, especially if they thought they were in a gang. I told him, "When I asked the officers about why they acted that way, they said "It helps create respect for police."   

I wondered how the Lieutenant felt about this. He replied "Police are scared just like everyone else. It's easy to cross over the line and become just like the gang members - intimidating, mouthing off, being aggressive."

In 1995, a new sheriff was elected to office in the Las Vegas area. Just a few years after he reorganized and focused his gang units efforts "Las Vegas gang control efforts encountered a major stumbling block. Two gang unit officers committed a drive-by shooting ... One of (the gang unit's) officers opened the side door of their van and shot six times into a crowd of young people on a street corner, killing one gang member." (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  107-108)

"... a still greater problem with loosely coupled gang units (gang units that are organizationally decoupled from the rest of their police department) is the potential for them to develop unique internal subcultures that can become at odds with the mission of the parent department, or even with the law itself." (Katz and Webb, 2004, p. 465)

The chorus of degradation represented by these incidents adds another element to the gang situation and, like the gangs themselves, needs to be corrected if we are to take steps to reduce gang activity. Having police act like gang members is not an acceptable, ethnical, legal, or logical way to handle the gang situation.

Field Note: The owner of a resort complex in Sarasota, Florida, when asked what he thought the answer to the gang situation was, said "Hiring more cops isn't the answer. They're too hard on these kids. I think they sometimes push them into gangs."

Whether police suspects make it to court or not is up to the local prosecutor, who may exercise considerable discretion on whether to bring any given case to court, plea bargain it, or not pursue the case at all.
