An Introduction to 
The Police Response to Gangs * 

Katz and Webb (Katz and Webb, 2004) believe there has been a shift in the way the gang phenomenon has been handled from a social interventionist approach to a strategy of suppression. They identified three reasons for that shift.

First, they found that many policymakers today "no longer believe that the social intervention approaches of the 1960s and 1970s are successful in dealing with gang problems." (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  15)  Second, they note that policymakers believe the nature of the gang phenomenon has changed over time. Today, gangs are perceived of as much more dangerous and their members responsible for disproportionately more crime, drug use, and drug dealing when compared to non-gang member delinquents. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  15) Finally, there is the "combined effect of disenchantment with social intervention strategies and increasing public acknowledgement that the gang problem has grown. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  15)

This may also be an appropriate place to raise the issue of the "fit" of a police gang unit's philosophy or approach towards its work within the larger context of the police organization's philosophy. Many police organizations today emphasize the need for community-policing - for creating partnerships with community residents, to gain their active support and cooperation. Most police gang units, on the other hand, are confrontation-oriented, secretive, and seldom seek cooperation from the community.

What are the implications of such an out-of-synch approach? Wouldn't a police gang unit be able to gather more intelligence if it had a better working relationship with neighborhood residents? Would that not lead to being more effective in reducing gang activity? Would it make sense to have at least one officer in a police gang unit be community-oriented and provide a face for the unit that allowed it to gather intelligence and share its intelligence with the community?

In every city I visited for this study I rode with police who had as their charge the oversight of gang activity. As may be expected, their response, and the response of their departments, to gangs and gang members varied widely. One of the most fascinating things I discovered was the process through which some departments passed on their way from denying there was a "gang problem" in their jurisdiction to actually doing something about it.
