Part 6:
Police Perceptions of the Gang Situation

I interviewed scores of gang unit police officers over the past three years. I felt they were very candid with me. So candid, in fact, that were I to publish their names along with their comments, some would have been put in jeopardy. For the most part, however, what they had to say they would have shared with anyone concerned enough to sit quietly and listen.

I've chosen a few of their comments from my field notes and tried to put them into meaningful categories, which is what you will find below. The comments are representative of the nearly 400 pages of single-spaced, typed field notes I collected from interviewing police.

Field Note: The gang unit specialist said "We are not overrun by gangs. Just look around. Most kids from elementary to high school are attending school, volunteering in the community and are good kids. If we talked about the one-per-centers as the proportion of kids who are in gangs, that would be a lot of kids!"  

Note: In fact, "one percenters" is a fairly accurate estimate of the American gang population. One percent of everyone in the United States 10 to 29 years of age (the most gang prone ages) on July 1, 1996, would equal 731,530 people. (Population Estimates Program, US Census Bureau, 2001)  In 1996 there were approximately 846,500 gang members in the United States. (Egley, 2000, page)

Police Perceptions of the Public

I sensed a great deal of frustration among police regarding community members who did not live in gang neighborhoods. Comments about their lack of knowledge concerning the gang situation, tendency to deny there is a problem, and attitude that "It's their (inner-city residents') problem, they need to fix it," were often heard.

Some police reflected upon society generally and its relationship with law enforcement.

Field Note: The gang unit supervisor feels that "There is a morale problem in the department, as in lots of other police departments. Society has less and less trust in the police. And our police administrators are more concerned about doing the politically correct thing that we don't get to do law enforcement as it should be done. There are limited resources and little public support. And we can't do our job if the public doesn't help. Gang work complicates this with long hours, sleepless nights, frustration and a lot of danger."

It also became obvious that many gang unit officers appreciated the bind in which most gang-dominated neighborhood residents found themselves.

Field Note: Driving through a gang-infested neighborhood the gang unit officer said "Most of these are good people, caught in this neighborhood and having to live with the gangs because they can't afford to get out and no one wants to buy in to the neighborhood."

They may have lived in the neighborhood for years, long before gangs dominated the streets. Now, unable to afford housing in outlying areas, and unable to sell their home, they are stuck. Officers also commented on difficulties related to residents' efforts to keep their children from becoming involved in gang activity and noted that, too often, they lose the battle.

Police Perceptions of Gang Neighborhoods

Although every officer I interviewed said gang members may live in any part of town - from wealthy neighborhoods to the "projects" - not once did they ride through an upper-middle-class or wealthy neighborhood in their normal round of duty while I was with them. I rather doubt they did so even when an observer wasn't in the car.

Field Note: When asked why the gang problem in town isn't worse, the gang unit officer said "The problem may not be worse than it is because the city doesn't have the large public housing projects like some other big cities."

The police I interviewed stereotyped people who lived in poor inner-city areas. They were often characterized as being uneducated and having values distinctly different from those held by people found in the middle- or upper class.

Field Note: The gang unit supervisor said "There is a completely different mentality among that (inner-city housing project) population. Ten folks might live in a building and none of them will carry their trash to the street for pickup. Instead, they let it accumulate on the back stairs of their flats. None of them would replace a broken window. Instead, they cover the space with a plastic sheet."

Behind some of the comments were apparently sympathetic observations about the disenfranchisement inner-city residents experience. 

Field Note: This gang unit officer described the neighborhood in which he works by saying "The residents are poor and uneducated, families have no father, the buildings are among the oldest in the city, the city does not give the neighborhood the kind of special attention it gives other neighborhoods, kids don't know who their fathers are, and many families are on welfare." I could hear the desperation in his voice.

In nearly every instance police recognized how the now-gang-dominated community had once been "a nice place to live." The only exception was in parts of Los Angeles where Hispanic gangs have existed for generations. None of the officers ever knew of those neighborhoods as being free of gangs.

Police Perceptions of Gang Members' Families

Another theme which ran through the comments made by gang unit officers was their identification of a lack of parental involvement or discipline as characteristic of the families living in gang-dominated neighborhoods. Officers expressed their concern that some gang members' families didn't know of their child's involvement in a gang and were unable to tell whether the child was in a gang or not.

Field Note: The gang unit officer told me "Parents are clueless about what their kids are up to. We find bandanas and gang graffiti in the kids' bedrooms and the parents don't know what they mean or that their kids are getting involved or are involved in a gang. They are simply naive about gangs."

There were, of course, officers who knew that some of the parents were involved in gangs and were simply pretending to be ignorant of their child's involvement. I found no police who could be easily fooled or mislead. Reality and too many disappointments had robbed them of any willingness to give people the benefit of the doubt.

Police Perceptions of Gangs

Some officers are more contemplative than others, studying the gangs with which they work and making observations that were insightful.

Field Note: This gang unit officer does not see gangs in this community as organized. Instead he characterized their members as having a "me mentality. They each seem to be out for their own thing." When asked why the gangs were not better organized he said "They consist of kids, most of whom are not disciplined, few members can express themselves well, and they're lazy."

Field Note: Another gang unit officer said "I think every gang member loses self-esteem when they get involved with a gang. When gangs first start up there's no leader. It doesn't take long before a leader emerges and, when he does, he starts telling the other members what to do. 

"Eventually, some of the gang members will run away from home because of the static they get there, or the abuse, or whatever, and find themselves living poor. The conditions are very bad - filth and hunger. They steal for the leader, beat up other people they are told to assault and eventually they get caught by the police. They're stigmatized, put in detention and this is how I see it in terms of them losing their self-esteem when they join up with a gang."

Some of the police I interviewed have had extended and numerous conversations with gang members and their relatives. During those conversations they learned a great deal about why some gang youth got into trouble.

Field Note: I asked the gang unit supervisor why youths of Mexican heritage are in gangs. He told me they are in gangs for a variety of reasons. "If the parents are illegals, then income is needed and the youths make money selling drugs. They have little education and don't know better and are not employable. Their parents may not be able to work and this also leads to youths joining gangs to make money. 

"And there are language barriers. If they can't speak English it's difficult to get legitimate work. And if they can speak English, but think they still have an accent, they may not use the English due to embarrassment. Mexicans who are born here tend to stick with their own and don't speak English."

They also learn a great deal about which gangs are not getting along with one another. In communities where gangs are divided along ethnic lines, the situation becomes complex.

Field Note: The gang unit commander said there are "Cambodians, Laotians, and Vietnamese gangs here. Very mobile, don't get along, highly organized, keep to themselves, and connected to their own kind across several cities. They ask permission from similar gangs before entering another city to commit crimes or pay tribute to the local gang/organization and return home. They seldom claim gang membership, have excellent weaponry, and they are very brutal."

In more advanced emerging- and chronic gang communities it was not uncommon to hear officers talk about how gangs which had previously had poor relations were now tolerating each other.

Field Note: The gang unit officer told me that over the last ten years "The gangs have started to work together - to cooperate with each other. There's more than enough drugs and more than enough customers and money to go around."

Police Perceptions of Gang Members

Dealing with them day in and day out, gang unit officers develop an attitude about gang members, as would anyone in the same situation. Some of them adhere to such adages as the following.

Field Note: Many gang unit officers told me "If it walks like a duck and talks like a duck, it is a duck. If a youth walks like a gang member and talks like a gang member, he or she probably is a gang member."   

Others take a more penetrating look.

Field Note: The gang unit supervisor said "Gang members don't want to rob and steal, they just want a challenge and this is the challenge" with which they are faced (because legitimate employment seems unobtainable). 

She told me a story about Mexican gang youth who "group together, get into an argument, one thing leads to another and someone is beaten and robbed. The robbery leads to continued fights, an arrest, an assault charge and one thing leads to another. It's the challenge that is relevant. If they had access to legitimate opportunities, they would take them on with the same enthusiasm."

Some officers have simply been around the gang situation for so long that there appears to them to be little which is new or unique. I had a similar feeling after just three years of part time involvement.

Field Note: Referring to gang members, the gang unit officer said "Same circus, different clowns."

As I spoke with my colleagues and others about my research I began to hear myself say "Different places, different faces, same problems."

Field Note: "The gang problem is not about a lack of opportunities," was an observation made by a senior officer in the gang unit. "I think the opportunities are there. The gang members are lazy. Sellin' drugs is easy."

While most people will never encounter dedicated, hard core gang members, gang unit officers encounter them daily. I believe this constant exposure creates and nourishes a sense of contempt and negativity toward gang members.

Field Note: An officer told me "Once someone's become a gang member, even by the age of twelve or thirteen, it's too late to talk about intervention. They're lost causes. A local black gang dressed a retarded boy in red and sent him out for some hamburgers. They sent him through rival gang territory just for fun."

Police Perceptions of Why Some Youths Join a Gang

The police gang unit officers I encountered expressed a wide range of beliefs as to why some youths join gangs. Their experience working with gang members has given them what seems to be a realistic outlook. The same was true for probation and parole officers who had specialized caseloads of gang members.

Explanations offered by police ranged from the impact of changes in the global economy on inner-city, ghetto youth to the effects of growing ethnic and racial diversity of the local population. None of the officers attributed gang behavior to biological or strictly psychological causes. Nearly all who cared to venture an answer pinned their explanations on social influences. Some, as seen below, dismissed the role of social influences and held the individual responsible for his or her behavior. 

Field Note: One of the gang unit officers asked me how I would explain the fact that, in a family with several children, one child becomes a doctor, another becomes a plumber, the third becomes a librarian, and one becomes a gang member and career criminal. "It's a matter of choice," he said, "it's not the family, it's not the environment and it's not biology. It's simply choice. Some of these kids make the wrong choices, while their brothers and sisters made the right choice."

Katz and Webb report that, in the four police departments they studied (Las Vegas, Inglewood/CA, Phoenix, and Albuquerque), officers "gave a common set of causal explanations for the gang problems in their communities. They emphasized cultural explanations ... at least some ... saw drug abuse at the root of the gang problem, and most saw poverty and related social conditions as contributing to the problem." (Katz and Webb, 2004, p. 323)

Among the social influences were all of those mentioned in the discussion of why gangs form. While some police seemed rather narrow-minded in their understanding of gangs and the people in them, most officers I interviewed had interesting and well-grounded explanations. Some, it seemed, were almost scholarly. This is not surprising given the high proportion of police today who have a college education and a genuine interest in understanding the problems with which they work every day.

Police Perceptions of Gang Crimes and Victims

Police perceptions of the crimes gang members commit are based upon the stark reality of what they see on the street and in hospitals every day.

Field Note: The Asian gang unit specialist told me "Asian gangs are into counterfeiting, home invasions of older local Asians, and extortion of local Asian-run businesses. They are the most likely to be homogeneous [allowing no other ethnicities into the gang] because of their cultural background and language. They are more secretive than most ethnic gangs and difficult to penetrate. Their crimes are more likely to go unreported due to their intimidation of their victims."   

He gave an example of what the local Asian gang members are doing. "A local Asian businessman refused to cash a counterfeit check printed by an Asian gang member. Printing counterfeit checks is one of 'their things.' He was taken off the street and tortured. The skin of one forearm was filleted and left hanging on his arm. He was put back on the street and, from that point on, no victim resisted cashing the bad checks. The Asian gang netted over $600,000 in this swindle before we were able to apprehend them."

The police I observed were sincere in their concern for the victims of gang-related crimes. They told me of the wounding, injury, and death of innocent bystanders and of threats made by gang members toward elementary school children who were simply trying to walk from their home to school and back. 

Field Note: An experienced gang unit supervisor said "There are gangs in every ethnic group in town and they each tend to victimize their own people in their own neighborhood."

Many of the officers spoke of the harm being done by gang members to members of their own gangs and of the brutality and abuse some gang members faced in their own homes. Most of the officers I interviewed had sympathy for the victims and the gang members, although their sympathy wore thin when the most dangerous and callous gang members were encountered.

Interestingly, Katz and Webb found a disjuncture between police perceptions of the gang situation in a given community and the perception of the gang situation by the residents of that community. "Overall, (local residents and other non-police personnel) differed from (police), in that they perceived a substantial gang problem in their communities. (Police) ... thought the problem was in decline. In contrast, (non-police) in the same communities reported that the gang problems were continuing and, in fact, were getting worse. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p. 368)

Police Perceptions of Schools

All the officers I interviewed had completed high school and most had a college degree. They had an appreciation for what an education has to offer and what schools have to put up with when it comes to juvenile delinquency and gangs.

Field Note: Talking about the schools and parents, the police gang unit member said "Truancy is a real problem in this city. We regularly carry out truancy sweeps. Some of them are at night. We see kids hanging around, question them about school and, if they are not attending, we bring them down to the station, cite them, call in their parents, tell them what's going on, ask them to do something positive about it, then release them all."

They appreciate the fact that their communities' teachers are faced with crowded classrooms and inadequate funding. They also realize that some of the children don't care about school due to parental non-involvement and the perception that they will have limited opportunities for employment following graduation. 

Field Note: A long-time gang unit officer said "The problem is that schooling doesn't really matter to the kids or their parents. I don't know about your schools, but ours are very overcrowded. Discipline is difficult and it's hard to figure out who is attending and who's not. And there's little incentive to go to school and finish since there's no job market out there for lots of these kids."

I was surprised to find that, in most of the inner-city areas I visited, student truancy was overlooked. Either the position of truancy officer did not exist (due to budgetary considerations, I was told), or they existed but were not fully functioning.

Field Note: "If a child is truant enough," the officer said, "especially in the high schools, the schools just take his name off the role." They simply disappear.

Police Perceptions of Social Service Agencies

I asked many of the police I interviewed what they knew about their local community-based treatment programs for youths involved in gangs or violence. Only a handful were aware of these programs, although they did know if their own department offered prevention or intervention programs. In most cases, the local community had several prevention and treatment programs designed for at-risk youths who might be heading into gang activity, but the police had little knowledge of them. From our conversations I gathered there was little in their training which informed them about such programs.

There was little hesitancy, however, when it came to identifying state-based youth- or family service agencies as being a problem.

Field Note: A gang unit officer said "Many of the more problematic children are sent to the Division of Family Services where, for the most part, they are shuffled around and basically thrown away."

It was dismaying to hear a steady stream of complaints about various state-run Family Services and Youth Services offices. For the most part, the complaint was that the state offered little in the way of effective treatment. Officers expressed their belief that state workers had excessive caseloads and were poorly paid and that these factors contributed to the problem.

Police Perceptions of Gangs and the Future

None of the officers I interviewed ever envisioned a day when gangs would be absent from the streets of their community. They viewed gangs as a necessary evil - something that's been around for a long time and will remain. Most guessed the gang situation would get worse in the future due to increasing diversity in the inner-city populations, the ever-widening divide between the rich and the poor, globalization and a concomitant loss of jobs or lowering of pay scales, society's increasing reliance on technology (resulting in fewer jobs for the poor, uneducated, and unskilled), and inner-city youths' continuing failure in school. It was a bleak picture, but it is supported by the findings of many social researchers who have reached the same conclusion.

Stereotypes, Police, and Gang Members

The term "stereotype" was used earlier in describing how some police viewed inner-city residents. Stereotypes are important to understand, particularly as concerns their impact upon those who harbor them and those upon whom they are imposed.

Stereotypes are defined as "unreliable generalizations about all members of a group that do not recognize individual differences within the group." (Schaefer, 2001, p. 259) I believe everyone uses stereotypes, at least until they get to know the people they are stereotyping and find the stereotype is useless. Police stereotype inner-city residents and inner-city residents stereotype the police. Both stereotypes are inaccurate and often lead to conflicts and misunderstanding.

I believe police stereotype gang members and gang members stereotype the police. I certainly heard enough stereotypical comments from each about the other to believe there are many misunderstanding between the two groups. There's another problem which arises from the use of stereotypes and it has a direct bearing upon the gang situation and police. Schaefer tells us "In certain situations, we may respond to stereotypes in such a way that false" stereotypes " end up being accurate. In this phenomenon, called the self-fulfilling prophecy, a person or group that is described as having particular characteristics begins to display those very traits." (Schaefer, 2001, p. 259)

If I stereotype gang members as aggressive, I will respond to a gang member as though he were aggressive. My response may elicit aggressiveness in the gang member. As Schaefer said, my response made the gang member behave aggressively and I can then say "See, I told you gang members were aggressive!" It is a self-fulfilling prophecy.

In this same fashion, I believe some police stereotype gang members as hostile or aggressive and, without provocation, respond to them as though they are going to be hostile and aggressive. The response they get in turn is just what they had anticipated, even though it didn't have to be that way. I witnessed this many times over the past three years. There were situations in which officers started their interaction with suspected gang members in a hostile manner. The situation quickly escalated as both the police officer and the suspected or real gang member became hostile and aggressive towards one another.

Field Note: I rode with two gang unit officers tonight - a male and a female. The female officer carried a knife - about 8 inches long, 2 inches wide. Strictly military. She said "I like the way it sort of keeps them (gang members) off balance. They don't know what to do with a cop with a knife. They know about the gun. That's one thing. But the knife ... it's something else to them. It confuses them. I like that." 

In the process of questioning two gang members that night the female officer threatened one of them saying "How'd ya like for me to stick this in your fuckin' heart and twist it around?" The gang member had not expressed anger or hostility towards the officer at any time that evening. I didn't understand where the officer's hostility and anger were coming from.

As on other occasions, I wondered if the stereotype had blinded all parties involved and simply made matters worse.

Two years after conducting my original field work, Katz and Webb conducted a study which, in part, looked at how police gang unit members viewed their role. They found that

Gang unit officers appeared united in the perception of themselves as crime fighters, regardless of their units' formal mandates. Our observations suggested that the gang unit officers perceived their units not as responding to crime, so much as responding to groups of individuals whom they believed to be deeply involved in criminal activities. Although this may bee a fine distinction, it is meaningful. Gangs and gang members, not just their criminal acts, were viewed by the officers as the threat, and gang unit officers believed that their mission was to protect the community by combating those gangs. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  246, italics in original.)

Despite the importance they attached to interagency cooperation, however, gang unit officers (in three of the four departments studied) rarely had contact with others outside their own units. (Katz and Webb, 2004, p.  249)
